Angkor Wat

The largest religious monument in the world and a symbol of Cambodia's rich heritage

A Masterpiece of Khmer Architecture

Angkor Wat, meaning "Temple City" or "City of Temples", is a temple complex in Cambodia and the largest religious monument in the world by land area. Built in the early 12th century by King Suryavarman II, it was originally constructed as a Hindu temple dedicated to the god Vishnu, but gradually transformed into a Buddhist temple by the end of the 12th century.

This magnificent structure is considered the apogee of the classical style of Khmer architecture and has become a symbol of Cambodia, appearing on its national flag. The temple is admired for the grandeur and harmony of its architecture, its extensive bas-reliefs, and for the numerous devatas adorning its walls.

Architectural Marvel

The temple complex spans over 400 acres and is designed to represent Mount Meru, the home of the gods in Hindu mythology. The central tower rises 65 meters above the ground and is surrounded by four smaller towers, creating a quincunx pattern.

The outer wall of the temple is surrounded by a moat that is 190 meters wide, creating a sense of isolation and grandeur. The temple's bas-reliefs stretch for nearly 800 meters and depict scenes from Hindu mythology, including the famous "Churning of the Ocean of Milk" scene.

Historical Significance

Angkor Wat was built during the height of the Khmer Empire, which ruled much of Southeast Asia from the 9th to the 15th century. The temple was constructed as the state temple and eventual mausoleum for King Suryavarman II, who reigned from 1113 to approximately 1150.

Unlike most Angkorian temples, Angkor Wat is oriented to the west, which has led many scholars to conclude that it was intended to serve as a funerary temple. The temple has remained a significant religious center since its construction, first Hindu, then Buddhist, and continues to be a place of worship today.

Visitor Tips

Best Time to Visit

Visit early in the morning (around 5:30 AM) to witness the spectacular sunrise over the temple. The golden hour provides the best lighting for photography and a more peaceful experience before the crowds arrive.

Tickets

Purchase your Angkor Archaeological Park pass in advance. Options include 1-day ($37), 3-day ($62), or 7-day ($72) passes. The ticket office is located on the road to Angkor Wat, and you'll need a passport photo.

Dress Code

Dress respectfully as this is an active religious site. Shoulders and knees must be covered. Wear comfortable walking shoes as you'll be exploring extensive grounds and climbing steep stairs.

Photography

Photography is allowed throughout the complex. The best shots are during sunrise and sunset. Be respectful of monks and worshippers, and avoid using flash in interior areas.
